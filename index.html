Who Gets Access?

Does household income predict whether young people in Texas go to college? And if income alone doesn't fully explain the gap, what else might?

Jonathan Cox is a sociologist and data analyst focused on education equity and social policy. This project uses publicly available Census data to surface patterns in who attends college across Texas counties.

Key Findings

What the data shows

Across 221 Texas counties with reliable population estimates, income and college enrollment move in the same direction — but income explains only a small fraction of why enrollment rates vary so dramatically across the state.

r = 0.20

Pearson correlation between county median income and enrollment rate. Statistically significant (p = 0.003), but income explains just 4% of the variation.

5.1 pts

Percentage-point gap in average enrollment between the highest and lowest income quartiles (29% vs. 24%). Real, but smaller than many would expect.

77%

Enrollment rate in Brazos County (home to Texas A&M), the highest in the state — driven by institutional presence, not household wealth.

3.8%

Enrollment rate in Mills County, a middle-income county (Q3) over 100 miles from the nearest university — suggesting geography matters beyond income.

Analysis

Income and enrollment: a weak but real relationship

The scatter plot below shows every Texas county as a single point. The trend line confirms that higher-income counties tend to have higher enrollment rates, but the relationship is loose. Counties cluster far above and below the line, meaning many other factors are shaping enrollment outcomes.

The most striking outliers are university host counties, labeled at the top of the chart. Brazos (Texas A&M), Erath (Tarleton State), and Nacogdoches (Stephen F. Austin) all enroll far more young people than their income levels would predict. At the other extreme, several rural counties in deep West Texas and the Panhandle sit at the bottom regardless of income.

Figure 1. College enrollment rate (ages 18–24) vs. median household income for 221 Texas counties. Points colored by income quartile. Labeled counties are the strongest residual outliers from the linear trend.

The quartile picture: a gap at the top, noise in the middle

When counties are grouped into four equal income tiers, the highest income quartile does enroll more students on average. But the Q2 and Q3 gap is essentially flat, and the error bars tell the real story: the spread within each quartile (up to 17 percentage points) is three times larger than the gap between them.

This pattern suggests that policy interventions targeting income alone would miss most of the counties with low enrollment. Within any income group, some counties enroll at much higher rates than others — pointing to factors like campus access, high school quality, and community norms that operate independently of household wealth.

Figure 2. Mean college enrollment rate by county income quartile. Error bars show ±1 standard deviation across counties within each quartile.

Where the access gap is most severe

The fifteen counties with the lowest enrollment rates are concentrated in West Texas, the Panhandle, and the Rio Grande Valley — regions with limited higher education infrastructure and significant distances to the nearest campus. Most of these counties are in the lowest income quartile, consistent with the correlation. But three are not.

Mills, Lipscomb, and Karnes counties all sit in Q3, with median incomes between $60K and $73K. Their appearance in the bottom fifteen is difficult to explain through income alone and points toward geographic isolation and institutional absence as independent barriers to access.

Figure 3. The 15 Texas counties with the lowest college enrollment rates (ages 18–24), colored by income quartile. Q3 counties in this list — Mills, Lipscomb, Karnes — are notable because their income levels do not predict this level of enrollment gap.

A state divided: the full distribution

The histogram of enrollment rates across all 221 counties reveals a right-skewed distribution: most counties cluster between 10% and 40%, with a long tail of high-enrollment counties pulled upward by university towns. The mean (26%) sits above the median (25%), a signature of that skew.

The shape of this distribution matters for policy. Interventions designed around the average county would miss the significant cluster of counties below 15%, where barriers to access are likely structural rather than marginal.

Figure 4. Distribution of college enrollment rates across 221 Texas counties. The right tail reflects university host counties; the dashed line marks the mean (26%).

Methodology

How this analysis was done

  1. Data collection. Two tables were downloaded from the U.S. Census Bureau's American Community Survey (ACS) 5-Year Estimates for 2024, covering the period 2019–2024: Table S1401 (School Enrollment) and S1901 (Household Income), both for all 254 Texas counties.

  2. Data preparation. ACS table exports are structured with counties as columns and demographic categories as rows — the opposite of a standard analysis-ready format. The raw files were reshaped, the two target variables were extracted (18–24 enrollment counts and median household income), and the tables were joined by county name.

  3. Data quality. Thirty-three counties with fewer than 200 residents aged 18–24 were excluded from statistical analysis; ACS estimates for very small populations carry margins of error larger than the estimates themselves. Fourteen counties where enrollment appeared as zero (likely a Census suppression artifact rather than a true count) were recoded as missing rather than zero.

  4. Analysis. Descriptive statistics, income quartile groupings, and a Pearson correlation were calculated across the remaining 221 counties. The ten lowest-enrollment counties were identified and their income levels examined.

  5. Visualization. Four charts were produced in ggplot2 to show the income–enrollment relationship, the quartile comparison, the counties with the worst access gaps, and the full distribution of enrollment rates statewide.
